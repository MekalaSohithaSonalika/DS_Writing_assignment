Global Coal Consumption Trends

Coal consumption grew by 1.8% in 2024, marking the third consecutive year of growth. This increase was primarily driven by emerging economies in Asia, particularly for electricity generation and industrial use.

It reveals a dramatic overall increase in total emissions, particularly accelerating from the mid-20th century onwards. While Land Use Change CO2 was the dominant emission source in the mid-19th century and saw a significant spike around the 1950s before declining, the profile has fundamentally shifted. By the end of the period, coal combustion clearly dominates, displaying exponential growth since the 1980s to become the largest single source by a significant margin. Methane emissions have also shown a substantial and consistent rise throughout the entire period, establishing themselves as the second-largest contributor. Oil consumption follows as the third-largest source, with steady growth beginning around the 1960s. Nitrous oxide emissions show a slower but steady increase, while contributions from natural gas and CO2 flaring remain comparatively minor throughout the depicted history. Overall, the graph highlights a major transformation towards an emissions profile heavily driven by fossil fuels, especially coal.

Global energy-related Greenhouse Gas Emissions

Annual CO2 Emissions (Million Tonnes)

The graph vividly portrays a dramatic surge in greenhouse gas emissions, largely driven by energy production and consumption. Initially, Coal and Oil dominated as the primary emission sources, likely due to the industrial revolution and widespread adoption of fossil fuels for energy generation. This dominance underscores the historical dependence on traditional energy sources and their significant contribution to emissions. Around the mid-20th century, a pivotal shift occurred with the rise of Natural gas as a significant contributor, further accelerating the overall emission increase. This could be attributed to the perceived advantages of natural gas as a cleaner-burning fuel compared to coal and oil. This shift reflects the changing dynamics of energy production and its impact on greenhouse gas levels. While emissions from Methane, Industrial processes, Nitrous oxide, and CO2 flaring also increased, their overall contribution remained comparatively lower, possibly due to their relatively smaller scale of use or implementation of emission control measures. The continuous and accelerating growth of greenhouse gas emissions emphasizes the urgent need for sustainable energy alternatives and effective emission reduction measures to mitigate the effects of climate change. This is because continued reliance on fossil fuels poses significant risks to the environment and global climate stability. The graph highlights the critical role of transitioning towards cleaner energy sources to ensure a sustainable future.

Annual CO2 Emissions (Million Tonnes)

"CO₂ Emissions in Trade" The graph depicts the trends in CO₂ emissions associated with trade activities for selected countries and the world from 1990 to 2021. A key observation is the significant increase in trade-related CO₂ emissions for China, surpassing all other countries and the world by a large margin. This could be attributed to China's rapid economic growth and its role as a major manufacturing and exporting hub. The United States initially had the highest emissions, but its growth rate slowed, and it was overtaken by China around 2005. India also shows a notable increase in emissions, although at a slower pace compared to China. Germany and the United Kingdom experienced relatively stable or slightly declining emissions. The overall trend indicates a continuous rise in global trade-related CO₂ emissions, highlighting the environmental impact of international trade and the need for sustainable trade practices. This emphasizes the importance of considering environmental factors in trade policies and promoting cleaner technologies to reduce emissions associated with global trade.

Temperature Change from Greenhouse Gases

Annual CO2 Emissions (Million Tonnes)

The graph reveals a continuous rise in global temperature since 1850, primarily driven by greenhouse gases. CO₂ emerges as the dominant contributor, with its impact escalating steadily and accelerating in recent decades. While methane and nitrous oxide also contribute to warming, their effect is less pronounced. A notable event is the sharp increase in temperature change observed from the late 20th century onwards, coinciding with increased industrialization and greenhouse gas emissions. The combined effect of all gases, depicted by the "Total GHG" line, further underscores the accelerating warming trend and its potential implications for climate change

Key Developments

  • China accounted for 54% of global coal consumption
  • Retirements of coal plants in OECD countries reached record levels
  • Coal prices averaged $120/tonne, down from 2022 peaks
  • Carbon capture projects at coal plants increased by 40%
